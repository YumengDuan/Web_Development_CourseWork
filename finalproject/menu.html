Solar Terms Chinese Names Date in General Meanings
Start of Spring lì chūn 立春 Feb. 3rd to Feb. 5th The Sun reaches 315° Ecliptic longitude. The first season in a Chinese year, the beginning of Spring. It represents growth and warmth in nature.
Rain Water yǔ shuǐ 雨水 Feb. 18th to 20th The Sun reaches 330° Ecliptic longitude. It means the start of rainfall. Rain is important for the growth of crops, and it is a reflection of the agricultural culture on the season.
Awakening of Insects jīng zhé 惊蛰 Mar. 5th to Mar. 6th The Sun reaches 345° Ecliptic longitude. It reflects the phenomenon of sprouting growth of natural beings affected by the change of biological rhythm. At this time, the temperature warms up, spring thunder stirs, rain increases, and everything comes to life from the cold winter.
Vernal Equinox chūn fēn 春分 Mar. 19th to Mar. 22nd The Sun reaches 0° Ecliptic longitude. This day the northern and southern hemispheres day and night equal division, since then the direct position of the sun continues to push from the equator to the northern hemisphere, the northern hemisphere around the day began to longer than the night.
Clear and Bright qīng míng 清明 Apr. 4th to Apr 5th The Sun reaches 15° Ecliptic longitude. This season reflects the changes in nature, and the spring scenery begins to appear. People clean the graves of their ancestors, plant trees, and start the new year of cultivation.
Grain Rain gǔ yǔ 谷雨 Apr. 19th to Apr. 21st The Sun reaches 30° Ecliptic longitude. At this time, rainfall increases significantly and cereal crops can thrive. This is the festival season that reflects the phenomenon of precipitation. It is customary for people to drink tea and appreciate flowers.
Start of Summer lì xià 立夏 May 5th to May 7th The Sun reaches 45° Ecliptic longitude. The sunshine increases, the temperature gradually rises, thunderstorms increase, and everything enters the peak growth stage. In many parts of China, there are customs such as eating “Summer rice", eating eggs and weighing.
Lesser Fullness (of Grain) xiǎo mǎn 小满 May 20th to 22nd The Sun reaches 60° Ecliptic longitude. Since then storms in southern China have begun to increase and wheat maturity in northern China has taken on a slight shape. The main folk customs of the season include the worship of the god through riding wooden cars to wish for harvest for this year.
Grain in Ear máng zhǒng 芒种 June 5th to June 7th The Sun reaches 75° Ecliptic longitude. This season is suitable for planting cereal crops with awn. It is also the cut-off point for the timing of planting crops, after which it becomes invalid. It is the time when southern China is planting rice and northern China is harvesting wheat.
Summer Solstice xià zhì 夏至 June 21st to 22nd The position of the sun reaches the northernmost point of the year - the Tropic of Cancer. At this time, the daylight hours across the Northern Hemisphere reach the longest of the year. After the summer solstice, the sun begins to move south. Since ancient times, Chinese folk have the custom of worshipping their ancestors on this season, and people will drink cold eat to avoid the heat.
Minor Heat xiǎo shǔ 小暑 July 6th to 8th The Sun reaches 105° Ecliptic longitude. Minor means it's not very hot yet, but it's about to reach what is the hottest and humid and muggy time of the year. Monsoon activity intensifies, but is favorable for crops. People in southern China will drink freshly brewed wine and eat dumplings in northern China.
Major Heat dà shǔ 大暑 July 22nd to 24th The Sun reaches 120° Ecliptic longitude. It is the hottest time of the year, hot, humid and rainy. Folk drink tea made from herbs, and there are customs such as burning joss sticks and drying out gingers for further uses.
Start of Autumn lì qiū 立秋 Aug. 7th to Aug. 8th The Sun reaches 135° Ecliptic longitude. Everything in nature begins to mature from luxuriant growth. Precipitation and humidity are at a turning point in the year and tend to decline. Autumn is the season of ripening and harvesting of grains. In ancient times, it was customary for Chinese people to worship the god of soil and celebrate a good harvest.
Limit of Heat chù shǔ 处暑 Aug. 22nd to Aug. 24th The Sun reaches 150° Ecliptic longitude. The direct sun point continues to move south, solar radiation weakens, subtropical high pressure also retreats to the south, the heat gradually disappears, the rainstorm weakens. Folkloric activities include duck eating, river lanterns, and herbal tea making.
White Dew bái lù 白露 Sep. 7th to 9th The Sun reaches 165° Ecliptic longitude. It is still hot with sunshine during the day, but the temperature drops quickly after evening. The temperature difference between day and night increases. The summer wind is gradually replaced by the winter wind, so the weather turns cooler. Local folklore will drink wine of grains to keep warm.
Autumnal Equinox qiū fēn 秋分 Sep. 22nd to 24th On this day the sun is at the equator and the day and night are of equal length around the globe. After that, the temperature drops everyday. This season is the traditional "Moon Festival", when people outside go home and eat moon cakes for family reunion.
Cold Dew hán lù 寒露 Oct. 7th to 9th This means late autumn. There is cold air coming south from the north and a large temperature difference between day and night. The rainy season ends in most parts of China. The traditional customs of cold dew mainly include appreciating maple leaves, eating sesame, eating crabs and drinking autumn tea.
Frost Descent shuāng jiàng 霜降 Oct. 23rd to 24th Late autumn scenes are evident, with cold air moving south more and more frequently. This is the time of the year when the temperature difference between day and night is the largest. There are mainly customs such as appreciating chrysanthemums (a flower), eating persimmons, and hiking.
Start of Winter lì dōng 立冬 Nov. 7th to 8th This is the beginning of winter, which means that everything enters a state of rest and recuperation. Winter is the season to enjoy the harvest and recuperate. It is also a very important festival in ancient China, with customs such as offering ancestor and feasting in some parts of China.
Minor Snow xiǎo xuě 小雪 Nov. 22nd to 23rd The light snow reflects the high frequency of cold wave and strong cold air activity, which means the weather will get colder and snowier gradually. People in northern China start to make preserved meat and people in Tibetan areas will taste patties.
Major Snow dà xuě 大雪 Dec. 6th to 8th Daxue marks the official start of the mid-winter season. Temperatures drop significantly and snowfall increases. Customs include viewing the closed rivers, pickling "salty goods" and eating red date cake.
Winter Solstice dōng zhì 冬至 Dec. 21st to 23rd The sun moves to the southernmost point - the Tropic of Capricorn - and it is the shortest day in the northern hemisphere. But the real bitter cold comes after this. The winter solstice is a very important festival. The customs vary in detail depending on the region of China.
Minor Cold xiǎo hán 小寒 Jan. 5th to 7th Cold air frequently moves south and temperatures continue to drop, but this is not the time for cold to reach its extreme. The very northern areas of China will experience colder in the Minor Cold than the Major Cold, but in most areas, it is not yet the coldest. People will eat glutinous rice in folklore.
Major Cold dà hán 大寒 Jan. 20th to 21st Major Cold means the weather is cold to the extreme. It is at the end of the year, after which a new cycle of the Chinese year begins. During this period, there are many important folk customs, such as replacing the old cloths with the new, eating preserved foods, and worshipping the land god.
